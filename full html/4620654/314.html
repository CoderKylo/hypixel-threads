Literature is often used as a form of expressing your emotion, talking about your trauma, or perhaps retelling a story from your own life, with some of the names and locations changed. One thing that commonly sinks into a writer's work is their political ideology, such as with J.B Priestley in his book “An Inspector Calls”. This impacted the story severely, and even though the play by itself doesn’t come close to being a long one, when you start to look deeper into its symbolism, that is where the tale becomes truly interesting.

Priestley wasn’t new to writing when he wrote “An Inspector Calls”. His career started off with two very successful novels, those being “The Good Companions” in 1929, and “Angel Pavement” in 1930. However, in 1934, John Priestley published “English Journey”, which was, unlike his prior titles, a travelogue, which documented the country during the Great Depression. This is an important point for our studies, as in this book we find that Priestley was a very left-leaning person, seeing the state of the country as a cause of the flawed capitalistic government. Nothing much happened in his life until World War Two. Priestley became a frequent broadcastor and talk show host on the BBC, with his talks reaching over 16 million people, with his only competitor being Winston Churchill, the prime minister of the UK. This success would not last long, as his talks were shortly cancelled for being too left-wing, and many feared that he had connections with the USSR and Joseph Stalin, even though he didn’t feel any sympathy towards the dictator. Nevertheless, this was the last straw for Priestley, as in 1945 he would release his most blunt critique of capitalism as a whole.
The story of “An Inspector Calls” begins far before the night of the inspection, and instead begins with the firing of Eva Smith, an employee of Birling and Co., owned by Arthur Birling. An interesting detail can be found just in the name of the company alone, as it pales resemblance towards the jewelry store Tiffany and Co. That store was originally called Tiffany, Young and Ellis when it was founded in 1837. Eventually, Charles Lewis Tiffany seized control over the company and changed its name, to show that his coworkers were no longer important, and that he was the man behind the jewelry giant. This shows that in the beginning, Arthur Birling was not in charge, and rather a co-owner, but he took control and got rid of his friends, who to him were enemies. This tiny detail sets up Mr. Birlings character as a cold, busy businessman, who doesn’t want anyone to interfere with his capitalistic practices. As the owner of a large company, made up from branches, which consisted of stores and factories, Eva Smith worked day and night in a unhygienic, cramped and dangerous factory, for 22 shillings and 6 pence, which was around 1 pound sterling, barely enough to survive. Birling once states that Eva was on the right track to receiving a promotion, which most likely means that she was very compliant and overworked, tired but still going, just to live another day. This behavior didn’t last, as she was one of the main organizers behind a strike at the company, only asking for a quarter of a pound raise. Sadly, the raise was cancelled and the young Eva Smith was now jobless. We already can see how Priestley’s socialistic beliefs have changed the story of the book, as we have seen an antagonised capitalist, a strike being put down quite violently, and an underpaid young soul losing their only source of income. This, however, does not come close to the first of the two main speeches in “An Inspector Calls”. Mr. Birlings speech.
We are now at a very rich estate owned by the Birling family, and are witnessing the engagement party. The two lovers, Sheila Birling and Gerald Croft are having the time of their lives, excited that they will be together in holy matrimony. This is where the fifty year old Arthur Birling decides to give a speech about how great life is. The young Croft is the heir to one of the main competitors of Birling and Co. Mr. Birling states that he hopes for the merging of the two colossal companies in the fictional town of Brumley, due to Sheila and Gerald getting married. He also says that we can put war behind us, that the economy is doing great and will be great forever, and that the Titanic will set sail soon, showing the great achievements of the capitalist system. This never happens. “An Inspector Calls”, while being written in 1945, takes place somewhere closer to 1912. This is Priestly using dramatic irony, as we know that all those claims are false, but Mr. Birling does not. This proves the fact that he is a greedy capitalist, as he claims capitalism will make everything better. But nothing stated in his long speech ever becomes the truth. The Great War, the Great Depression, the sinking of the Titanic, even his daughter's marriage all never happen. While the Birlings are having fun, cheerfully dining together, we are introduced to a new character, one that we know from the title.
Inspector Goole is one of the most interesting metaphors ever written. He is meant to represent both the readers and the Birlings conscience, while also being a in universe J.B Priestley. His role is simple: make everyone admit to their involvement in the situation regarding Eva Smith, also known as Daisy Renton. We have already analyzed Arthur’s firing of the girl, and we must now go through Gerald Croft, Sybil Birling, Sheila Birling and Eric Birling. Chronologicaly, after her firing from Birling and Co. Eva Smith changes her name to Daisy Renton, wanting to start anew, without her previous work complications. She miraculously avoids getting thrown in prison, and gets hired as an employee in the posh clothing store Milwards. Eva’s happiness in finding a new job does not last forever, as like a dog chasing a squirrel to eat it, Daisy is being, metaphorically, chased by the Birlings. She encounters the young Sheila, who is doing some light shopping. Daisy has just tried on a dress, and the rich, slightly spoiled, self conscious daughter of a capitalistic tyrant goes to try it on after her. Shocked, she admits to looking worse than the Milwards employee. Sheila manages to hear some giggling and snickering from Eva, whom she thought was ridiculing her, which makes the young Birling mad. Storming towards the store manager, Sheila demands for Daisy Renton to be fired from the store, and if the manager does not comply, neither her or her family shall ever shop in Milwards again. This demand, because it was set by the daughter of what a poorer citizen can only describe as a form of god, is met. Unlike her father, Sheila feels extreme guilt and understands her wrongdoings, even getting angry at her parents for being so cold to the girl. This also shows a new conflict within the family. The new generation wants to embrace socialism, while the older want to remain in power. This is seen continuously throughout the book and is a reference to a lot of socialist propaganda. Many left-wing politicians, writers etc. have stated that if the children and teenagres don’t revolt, nothing will ever change, no reforms will ever happen. Unlike those statements, after hearing the name Daisy Renton, we have to see the biggest change of the book thus far.
Gerald Croft was born into nobility. Unlike the hard headed Mr. Birling, who might have only received a slight amount of money from his parents, Gerald is sitting upon a golden throne, not yet realising what dangers it possesses. He is revealed to have had an affair with Daisy, which luckily never ended in them sharing a kiss or making love. Gerald had found the young, unlucky woman in a bar, working as a “woman of the night”. After saving her from a purple-faced womanizer, he offered Eva a short stay in an apartment which belonged to his friend, who gave Gerald the keys to said apartment before leaving on a short holiday. The young nobleman manages to make Daisy Renton not only survive, but also make her quite happy. His confidence in telling the story is nonexistent however, seemingly the charming and confident Croft has now lost all of his energy, and is hiding away from the knives of swords being thrown at him. After admitting to having an affair, we have to see Sheila, the love of his life, someone both he hoped he would marry, giving him back the wedding ring given to her at the dinner. Sheila never resents him, however, and instead is very understanding. While only being a minor character story wise, Gerald serves a much larger purpose when regarding the political message of “An Inspector Calls”. Gerald was always presented as quite handsome, featuring facial hair and an up right, straight posture. He was meant to be the sort of idealized plus one to Mr. Birlings capitalist speeches and remarks, acting as a sort of echo. If Arthur said that there would be an eternity of peace, Gerald would agree. He was meant to poke fun of the bubble of capitalism and capitalist culture, showing that socialism was more realistic. While the happy fantasy of the elder Birling were fighting the gruesome reality, the story takes a dark turn, and while this is the last event in the book, it has to be mentioned now.
A young man stands, with his suit and hair ruined, facing his family, who have found out about his involvement with Eva Smith. Eric Birling is the most interesting character in this book. He is the wildcard, the choleric, the troubled one. His story is similar to that of Geralds. He finds Daisy in the Palace Bar, the same place as she was before, and paid for a couple of drinks for the both of them. They left quite late, both drunk, and headed towards Daisy’s house, which was most likely the apartment of Geralds friend. This is where Eric commits a horrible crime, and forces himself upon the reluctant Daisy, ****** her. Somehow, the possibly numb, depressed woman does not resent him, and they meet once more, which again ends in them making love, at least this time consensually. It isn’t long before Eric learns of his lover's pregnancy, which prompts him to go as far as to steal from his father to try and make Daisy and his kid live a good life. He sadly has to face the rules set by the child's mother. Eva knows that Eric does not probably love her, and she wants true love in her life, even knowing that she has a person who stole from their own father and tried to help raise the kid. Her purity in love is a mistake that will have its consequences later. Eric is another socialist allegory, this time representing the troubled teenagers, the very revolutionaries meant to push the hammer and sickle forward, fighting against his capitalist authority, wanting to make the working classes, here Eva’s, life better.
Eric lashes out at his parents for being so caught up in their bubble that they fail to see how all the poor, lonely people live. He is enraged at how he couldn’t help raise the kid, who couldn’t just be aborted, as that would be illegal. Eva’s story at this point was horrible, after two firings and an unwanted kid she felt horrid, yet after receiving help and love from both Gerald and Eric, she felt that one day she might be happy. Yet, there was one person, colder than a kelvin zero, that would ruin her chances at redemption.
Sybil Birling. A woman whose deeds are by far the worst out of the ones we’ve mentioned. She is part of a charity committee in Brumley, who gives out money to help those in need. Eva, hoping to have a new start with the charity she was going to recieve, wanted to honor the father of her child, and introduced herself as Mrs. Birling, which was a fatal mistake. The actual Mrs. Birling took this as an insult, an attempted impersonation and an attack on her honor and the honor of her family, so she decided to take revenge by denying any form of charity be given to Eva. This broke her heart and presumably shortly after, Eva Smith was found dead. Sybil’s role in “An Inspector Calls” is not as simple as you may think. Why yes, she is similar to Arthur, being a ruthless capitalist authority figure, she also represents the inequality of the world. By denying a poor Eva payment, she keeps the money for other people who to her are more valuable, or perhaps for herself. Sybil represents the illusion of freedom that capitalism gives people, the satisfaction of money being received. After a long night of fighting, admitting, crying and screaming both the Birlings, as well as the inspector are tired. But the Birlings continue, which leads to the second of the two very important speeches. Inspector Gooles speech is both a synopsis of what happened tonight, a time of self reflection for the family members, and also the main message of socialist ideas. Goole remarks that there are millions of John Smiths and Eva Smiths in the world, and that we must help each other live together in unity, in a classless society. This is most likely J.B Priestley speaking through the character he made for himself to use as a vessel to make the exaggerated and antagonized capitalist caricatures realise that socialism is superior in all regards. After this speech he leaves, which ends his purpose. We are left with the Birlings, a family who may have changed, if not for Gerald returning back to the estate, to announce that Inspector Goole isn’t a registered police inspector. Many rejoice, yet not for long, as Edna, the Birling’s housemaid comes to announce that the police are coming and are inquiring about a girl that just died a couple of minutes ago. That girl was Eva Smith.

The story of “An Inspector Calls” follows a group of people in the midst of a tragedy that will change their lives, fighting to see who can last through better. We have the young socialists, admirable in their fight against the older generation of capitalists. The young are helped by a sort of messiah, a form of government, an authority who is respected enough to lead the young to victory. This is the story of not only this three act play, but also countries such as the USSR and the People’s Republic of China. We have very visible socialist themes which impacted the story. If the book was to be written by a capitalist, a liberitarian, or even an anarchist, the characters, story and motives would be different. In the end, it didn’t impact Priestley’s life severely, as even after being put on Orwell’s list, he faced little controversy. He died peacefully, knowing that he will be placed in his red heaven forever.

If the book was to be written by a capitalist, a liberitarian, or even an anarchist, the characters, story and motives would be different. In the end, it didn’t impact Priestley’s life severely, as even after being put on Orwell’s list, he faced little controversy. He died peacefully, knowing that he will be placed in his red heaven forever.
