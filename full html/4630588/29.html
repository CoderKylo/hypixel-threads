Honesty is a virtue that encompasses telling the truth, even when not prompted, and not omitting information, either. Honesty is closely tied in with trust, and integrity. An honest person does nothing to mislead others, and is forthcoming when they are responsible and something goes wrong. An honest person will also not embellish statements for personal gain. Honesty, as a principle, can benefit someone by allowing the person who is honest to have a clean conscience, knowing that they are not responsible for the creation and knowing distribution of lies. Honesty has long been valued, as it is a sign that someone will stick to their word, and is not malicious or hiding ulterior motives. Society as a whole benefits from honesty, as people only receive a true account of an event, and hear true statements from leaders and one another. Honesty also ensures that the systems set in place work smoothly. All chains of command rely on someone being honest to their superiors or to the people they manage/represent. When courts operate, they have all who speak swear to tell the truth (being honest), the whole truth (no omitting), and nothing but the truth (no embellishments). This swearing of an oath to being honest and true allows for completely fair hearings, so long as there is not a dishonest person perjuring.

An honest person is also sincere in their statements, not only saying what is true and noble, but what they genuinely believe. There is no falsifying beliefs or hiding facts for material, emotional or other gains. An honest person is one of the best friends one can ask for, as they will provide fair, accurate advice, and will tell you when you do something they are not comfortable with. An honest person is also direct, they do not try to hide things that are bad with flowery terms. They do not use jargon someone would not understand, unless it were relevant to a profession (ie using fancy medical terms, and then explaining it in layman's terms). They make sure they are able to be understood, and use accurate words to describe their actions, good or bad.

The opposite of honesty is dishonesty, which can be categorized into lying and insincerity. The dishonest individual will manipulate information, falsify information or omit information for their gain. They will mislead people in order to hide their wrongs, and are a moral detriment to society. Corrupt police officers, lying lawyers, and politicians who break their promises are all examples of dishonourable individuals, people you would not want in a position of authority. Similarly, friends who are insincere are not people one wants as friends either. If a dishonest person will only hang out with someone because they are popular, or wealthy, or otherwise considered attractive to be around, and they think they can gain from the relationship without telling the friend that is the only reason they are their friend, it leads to dysfunction, disappointment and anger when the liar is inevitably called out. Also, dishonest people have a negative impact on the overall trust level of society, as the more dishonest people there are, the less honest people will trust their fellow man.

Some examples of how honesty can improve your life are:
1.In relationships: Being sincere and not putting on an act leads to a deeper, closer relationship with whoever you are engaging with. This honesty will help you express who you are as a person, and learn more about the other person you are talking to. It also provides a stronger foundation for when hard times hit, as even when other issues arise, you will not be known as a liar.
2. As a member of society: If you are known to be true in your speech, more people will trust you, and take you seriously. If you embellish claims or omit information, people may label you as only trying to be provocative, and dismiss what you are saying as entertainment or lies.
3. Mental comfort: When something is wrong, you will admit it and suffer some short term stress, but you will have the comfort of knowing you have done the right thing, and not have a lie weigh down your conscience. Also, many self doubts are caused by lies you have been told, usually by yourself or others who attacked your insecurities. When you realize you are not irredeemable, or always weak, or worthless, you can work on improving yourself to be more the person you wish to be.
4. Solidity in beliefs: When you are honest with others, and yourself, you will find it easier to understand and embrace your beliefs. Without lies that make you comfortable in your current position clouding your memory, you can enact change when needed, and feel better about yourself.
5. If you go into a position of authority, whether it be as a doctor, lawyer, politician, legislator, teacher, or so on, you will be more deserving of the position and recognized as a legitimate authority if you are honest. Being dishonest in any of those positions can lead to repercussions, or you could be knowingly misleading people, which does not weigh on your conscience well.

Examples you can act honestly today:
1. If you have anything weighing on your heart, such as how you stole a toy in 4th grade or something, for example, be honest about it to whoever you wronged, and ask for forgiveness. Showing you will take responsibility for your actions and admit your faults is a good gauge of the honesty of an individual.
2. Speak only what you know to be true in public. Do not make things up for entertainment value, only explain things as they are. This is especially important in regards to religion, politics, the media and so on. Do not purposely spread things you know to be false, and do not create false things unless you explicitly label it as satire. This can help you avoid a defamation suit, and it will again show that what you say is to be taken seriously and be considered trustworthy by others.
3. Look into your mind and identify any negative beliefs about yourself. Ask yourself, are they true? If they are not, tell yourself that you are not those things, and be honest with yourself. If you are what you are accused of being, listen to your conscience and understand you must change that aspect of your life. For example, if you are overweight, or you do not exercise, or you are addicted to something, do not listen to the voice that says "this is fine". Change the behaviour and tell yourself "This is not fine, but I can work to be in the condition that I am in good health and be fine then".
4. Reflect on your own beliefs. How many of them are based on nonsense, or prejudice, or that you have adopted from an unhealthy mindset? If you have these beliefs, or are unsure of their true value, look at why you believe these things, go to the sources of your beliefs, and look at other beliefs if you feel you have no legitimate grounds to call these beliefs true.

I have tried my best with this explanation in the time given, I could have spent a bit longer but this is quite accurate on what I believe truth to be. I hope this guide can help you be more honest in the future, and limit the number of lies and half truths in this community and society at large.

I'd like to add an addendum. An honest person, when they realize something they have shared is false, or that they were wrong about something, will admit that. They will not allow it to linger, but rather fix the error they have made.

Honesty is a virtue that encompasses telling the truth, even when not prompted, and not omitting information, either. Honesty is closely tied in with trust, and integrity. An honest person does nothing to mislead others, and is forthcoming when they are responsible and something goes wrong. An honest person will also not embellish statements for personal gain. Honesty, as a principle, can benefit someone by allowing the person who is honest to have a clean conscience, knowing that they are not responsible for the creation and knowing distribution of lies. Honesty has long been valued, as it is a sign that someone will stick to their word, and is not malicious or hiding ulterior motives. Society as a whole benefits from honesty, as people only receive a true account of an event, and hear true statements from leaders and one another. Honesty also ensures that the systems set in place work smoothly. All chains of command rely on someone being honest to their superiors or to the people they manage/represent. When courts operate, they have all who speak swear to tell the truth (being honest), the whole truth (no omitting), and nothing but the truth (no embellishments). This swearing of an oath to being honest and true allows for completely fair hearings, so long as there is not a dishonest person perjuring.

An honest person is also sincere in their statements, not only saying what is true and noble, but what they genuinely believe. There is no falsifying beliefs or hiding facts for material, emotional or other gains. An honest person is one of the best friends one can ask for, as they will provide fair, accurate advice, and will tell you when you do something they are not comfortable with. An honest person is also direct, they do not try to hide things that are bad with flowery terms. They do not use jargon someone would not understand, unless it were relevant to a profession (ie using fancy medical terms, and then explaining it in layman's terms). They make sure they are able to be understood, and use accurate words to describe their actions, good or bad.

The opposite of honesty is dishonesty, which can be categorized into lying and insincerity. The dishonest individual will manipulate information, falsify information or omit information for their gain. They will mislead people in order to hide their wrongs, and are a moral detriment to society. Corrupt police officers, lying lawyers, and politicians who break their promises are all examples of dishonourable individuals, people you would not want in a position of authority. Similarly, friends who are insincere are not people one wants as friends either. If a dishonest person will only hang out with someone because they are popular, or wealthy, or otherwise considered attractive to be around, and they think they can gain from the relationship without telling the friend that is the only reason they are their friend, it leads to dysfunction, disappointment and anger when the liar is inevitably called out. When you realize you are not irredeemable, or always weak, or worthless, you can work on improving yourself to be more the person you wish to be.
4. Solidity in beliefs: When you are honest with others, and yourself, you will find it easier to understand and embrace your beliefs. Without lies that make you comfortable in your current position clouding your memory, you can enact change when needed, and feel better about yourself.
