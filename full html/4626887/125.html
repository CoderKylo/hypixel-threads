1. Accepting guilt or acknowledging your wrongs: The first step to forgiveness is recognizing the issue at hand. Accepting responsibility for all reasonably damaging behaviour (you do not have to apologize for mere opinions, like how you like to have your bread toasted, or an inconvenience, but you do for anything that causes harm) shows you are at least recognizing you have done something that has led to a dispute or created an offence.
2. Empathizing with the victim: Recognizing the damage, whether it be financial, physical, emotional and so on, will show that not only does the offender recognize their actions were wrong, but understands the damage the actions caused. This shows the victim that the offender has some empathy and wishes to restore things if possible.
3. Providing solutions to fix the problem and mend the relationship: If someone steals something, a sign they are sincere is to return the object, and say they owe the victim a favour. That way, it is more than just "I was caught, have your stuff back" and more "I wish to repair the wrong I have done, not just by returning something, but adding more to your life from this interaction". An example of this from the Bible is Zaccheus (can't spell) the tax collector, who took extra from the victims over time. He later repented, and not only returned all the money, but he gave back 4x what he stole, and gave half of his belongings to the poor. A more contemporary example of what you may see in the modern day would be someone promising to repair behaviours after recognizing they are self centred or manipulative, and promising to listen to how other people feel, and put the other person's interests first.
4. Asking explicitly for forgiveness: When someone is genuine in their apology, they will ask for forgiveness. This shows that they want a clear answer on the issue, and will not settle for it being left in the air, unresolved. A healthy apology also will not pressure for forgiveness if the person wronged does not wish to make a decision yet. It is asking and accepting the response graciously that makes it a good apology, regardless of whether it is "I forgive you", "I do not forgive you" or "Give me some time to see if you are being genuine".
5. Asking the person wronged for input: After proposing how they can fix something, the person who wronged the other should ask the person wronged what they think is a fair punishment or path to rehabilitation. Not all the demands must be met, if they are mean spirited or done spitefully, like someone saying they would want to beat you up or something. However, the person wronged has a different perspective and can point out problematic patterns in your behaviour, like how you swear before you start to get physically aggressive, or how you subtly pressure people to do what you want, without their best interests in mind (using the hypothetical "you", not you explicitly). Once this is factored into a path forward, you can reach the final steps.
6. Having closure: If both parties are willing to continue the relationship, then it can be as simple as "Thank you <name>, I'm glad we sorted that out, it has been on my conscience for a while/I know I was wrong. I hope we can have a long friendship and grow from this experience." If there was something more serious, like cheating in a relationship or a severe assault or something criminal, and one or both parties want to separate, a good apology (and acceptance of an apology) will respect the desire to no longer be in communication with the other party. Respecting boundaries, whether in a relationship or outside of one, is important. Don't throw a fit, or call names, or threaten someone who wants to leave. Also, do not tolerate someone doing that to guilt you into staying. If someone does and it isn't a clean break, a simple "I am sorry you aren't willing to accept the break. It is for the best, for both of us". This is one of the few times a "I'm sorry you" is okay, as it is not an apology for a wrong, but a recognition that someone is going through something necessary but uncomfortable. It is merely something the other person will have to process and grow from without you there, so it is at its finality, as far as you are concerned.
7. Tone and timing: Having the right demeanour when apologizing is important. Be empathetic, kind, stoic, firm in knowing what you did. Do not be arrogant, condescending, excessively sad and full of self pity, or otherwise expressing negative emotions on the person wronged. It is possible to feel some grief, especially if the wrong is a large one, and the relationship is someone close. However, it should be more of a remorse for the actions briefly transitioning into redemption, and not a long slog of pain and tears. Understand the trouble, do not get mired down in it. As for timing, if you have done something immediately damaging, give it a short bit of time and make sure you are genuine in your apology. If you were caught doing something in the shadows, you may need to give the person wronged a while to process it, especially if it is big. An example of that would be cheating. An instant apology would look insincere, as it looks like you were apologizing only because you were caught and wish to preserve the relationship. However, a quick apology (within 5-20 minutes of a minor issue) is appropriate if there is no major damage. Shock will fade pretty quickly, and if it is as simple as calling someone a name, apologizing right away is best. This way, the person wronged doesn't become bitter or resentful, harbouring anger toward you. Proper timing can be a make it or break it between seeming like you don't care, are lying to get someone to go away, or are genuinely sorry.

Let's have an example. Jimmy insulted Tom's girlfriend in a joke gone too far. It's been about 10 minutes, and the girlfriend left the room.

Jimmy: Hey Tom, I'm sorry I insulted your girlfriend. I should not have said she looked like a balloon. I know you like your girlfriend, and I did not mean to cause any distress to her, or you. I was merely trying to make a joke, but it fell flat, and it only hurt her, and you. I will be more considerate of others and not make jokes about sensitive topics like your girlfriend or her appearance again. I won't make fun of anyone's appearance, for that matter. Will you forgive me?
Tom: Yes, Jimmy, I can forgive you. I am happy you are taking our emotions into consideration. I now know it wasn't an insult, which makes me feel better. I was worried you were trying to be malicious.
Jimmy: Say, Tom, is there anything else I should do to fix the problem?
Tom: Now that you mention it, you apologized to me, but I don't think you've apologized to my girlfriend yet. Come with me, you can apologize to her and I'll take you to a coffee shop where we can read this comedy handbook my dad gave me.
Jimmy: Sure thing Tom, I'll be sure to apologize to her right away. I'll make sure I mention I wasn't trying to be mean, but I'll be more considerate in the future, and not make any jokes at her expense.
Tom: That sounds great, now come on. Let's hang out like we did before this happened.

Here, Jimmy is direct, considers emotions, provided a solution, accepted he was wrong, said what exactly was wrong about what he did, asked for input on how he could fix it, accepted feedback and reached an agreement to improve his conduct. After that, he and Tom hung out, showing that it was a positive experience for them to grow from.
