Arguments -
  1. We were pouring in too many resources - both human and financial - into an unpromising war.
(Economic) Since our involvement in 2001, the U.S. has spent over 2.3 trillion dollars in the Afghanistan war alone, according to data collected by independent researchers at Brown University. This is contributing to about 8.1% of our current 28.43 trillion dollars of national debt (Statista.com). The perceived cost of interest alone by 2050 could have been 6.5 trillion if this war were to have continued on its current path. To continue pouring money into this war would lead to an increase of the national debt, and the possibility of the U.S. defaulting on said debts. The result of that happening would lead to financial insecurity to the majority of U.S. citizens, leading to an overall decrease in the quality of life for the average U.S. citizen, among other economic issues.
(Human) In addition to the staggering economic cost, there is also the human price. According to the Associated Press (AP), over 172 thousand people in total have died as a result of direct conflicts from both sides of this war. This includes Taliban casualties, as well as civilian and U.S. military losses. I believe every life is worth counting, regardless of political association or heinous acts carried out by them. If this war were to continue, these numbers could have doubled - tripled, even - in a matter of years as weapons continued to evolve, and tactics continue to improve. Why should we force ourselves to see these numbers climb without any other major progress taking place?

  1. Previous global superpowers have invaded Afghanistan before, and failed to establish permanent change - we were not any different. There is a reason why Afghanistan is referred to as the “Graveyard of Empires”. The amount of times Afghanistan has been infiltrated, then subsequently those invaders have failed to promote permanence pertaining to particular beliefs, ideologies, or long-lasting government systems is unprecedented. History has shown time and time again that whenever Afghanistan was invaded with the intention to cause change - such as changes in national religion, ideologies, industrialization, or type of government - they would always fail the test of time and become rejected, or cause rebellious insurrections, by the Afghan natives1. Take what happened with that Afghan government during our time there, and right after we left. There was corruption everywhere, with little attempts from the Afghan people to change that. And right after we left, what happened? The corrupted government and military immediately fled from this system we invested so much money into. All of that money and time immediately was in vain the moment they abandoned their posts, further justifying our withdrawal. The U.S.’s involvement with instating a new government and integrating Western ideologies into it was destined for rejection by the native Afghan people - our ideologies and their culturalism are just too different for one to be integrated into the other. To continue attempting to change their ideological ideas and sway their views on government would have only ended in their rejection, like it has time and time before. We speak of how we should learn our history in order to avoid repeating it - and I ask, how is this any different?






For these reasons, I urge you to vote pro.
